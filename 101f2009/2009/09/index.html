Soundness and Validity

By David Sanson on September 25, 2009 10:27 AM | No Comments

We quickly went over the definitions of argument, premise, conclusion, soundness, and validity in class. Here they are again:

Definitions

  • An argument is a series of statements, one of which is the conclusion, the others, the premises.
  • An argument is valid if and only if it is impossible that both all of its premises are true and its conclusion is false.
    • i.e., there is no possible situation that makes all the premises true but makes the conclusion false.
  • An argument is sound if and only if it is valid and all of its premises are true.
  • note: given the definition of validity, a sound argument must have a true conclusion. That’s what’s great about sound arguments: they establish the truth of their conclusions.

Examples

Here are some examples:

  1. God exists.
  2. If god exists, then life is meaningful.
  3. Therefore, life is meaningful

This argument is valid. It is not clear whether or not it is sound: that depends on whether or not (1) and (2) are both true.

  1. Humans have two legs.
  2. Birds have two legs.
  3. Therefore, anything that has two legs is either a human or a bird.

This argument has true premises, but it is not valid, and so not sound.

  1. Apes have two legs.
  2. Birds have two legs.
  3. Therefore, apes and birds are two different kinds of animals.

This argument has true premises and a true conclusion, but it is not valid, and so not sound.

  1. The moon is made of green cheese.
  2. All things made of green cheese do not smell good.
  3. If something is visible in the night sky, it smells good.
  4. Therefore, the moon is not visible in the night sky.

This argument is valid, but premises 1 and 3 are false (2 is, I suspect, controversial), so it is not sound.

  1. Whales are fish.
  2. Whales are not fish.
  3. Therefore, life is meaningful.

This argument is valid(!), but it is not sound, since premise (1) is false. (Why is this argument valid?)

First Assignment

By David Sanson on September 24, 2009 4:30 AM | No Comments

Instructions

Answer the following prompt in 330 words or less: double-spaced, 12 pt serif font, 1” margins. Due in class on Tuesday, Sept. 29th.

In Part 4 of A Confession, Tolstoy says:

I could give no reasonable meaning to any single action or to my whole life. I was only surprised that I could have avoided understanding this from the very beginning - it has been so long known to all. Today or tomorrow sickness and death will come (they had come already) to those I love or to me; nothing will remain but stench and worms. Sooner or later my affairs, whatever they may be, will be forgotten, and I shall not exist. Then why go on making any effort? … How can man fail to see this? And how go on living? That is what is surprising! One can only live while one is intoxicated with life; as soon as one is sober it is impossible not to see that it is all a mere fraud and a stupid fraud! That is precisely what it is: there is nothing either amusing or witty about it, it is simply cruel and stupid.

Part I

Reconstruct the argument of this passage: identify and clearly state the conclusion; identify and clearly state the premises, including any “hidden” or implicit premises: you should not present it as a one premise argument! There is more to it than that.

Part II

Assess the argument. Is it valid? If not, explain how the premises can be true and the conclusion false. Is it sound? If not, either explain why it is not valid, or explain why one of the premises is false.
