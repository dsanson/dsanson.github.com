Topics

By David Sanson on October 27, 2009 5:07 PM | No Comments

You have a choice between the following three topics. Write on one of them: no more than 1000 words; due November 10th in class.

  1. On p. 217 of “Free Agency”, Gary Watson says

One job that Frankfurt wishes to do with the distinction between lower and higher orders of desire is to give an account of the sense in which some wants may be said to be be more truly the agent’s own than others…

On the bottom of p. 218 and top of 219, he says

What this difficulty shows that the notion of orders of desires or volitions does not do the work Frankfurt wants it to do. It does not tell us why or how a particular want can have, among all of a person’s “desires,” the special property of being peculiarly his “own.”

Explain what the difficulty is that Watson has described for Frankfurt’s view, and assess whether or not it shows what Watson says it shows. Explain how Watson’s view attempts to avoid this difficulty and assess its success at doing so.

  1. In “The Mystery of Metaphysical Freedom”, p. 460–61, Peter Van Inwagen says

As Carl Ginet has said, our freedom can only be the freedom to add to the actual past—for the past is unalterable; it is what we find ourselves with in any situation in which we are contemplating some course of action. […] And, unless we are bona fide miracle workers, we can make only such additions to the actual past as conform to the laws of nature.

In “Are We Free to Break the Laws?”, David Lewis argues that we are, in a sense, free to break the laws, admits that this requires a small miracle, but denies that it requires that we be miracle workers. Explain Lewis’s view and how it allows him to answer Van Inwagen’s challenge. Assess its plausibility.

  1. According to Roderick Chisholm, freedom requires that agent causation. Explain his view, being sure to explain what agent causation is, how it differs from event causation, and how it differs from the lack of causation. Assess the plausibility of the view.
