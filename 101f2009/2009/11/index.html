SEIs

By David Sanson on November 30, 2009 12:14 PM | No Comments

It is time for you to evaluate me. These evaluations are important: I use them to try to figure out how to do things better; they also play a role in tenure and promotion decisions. (I’ll be coming up for tenure in two years, so this looms large for me.) I appreciate the time you spend doing this.

I’m told that you can access the rating form by visiting your class schedule for this term and clicking the “Evaluation” button next to our class. (I think that this page should also get you where you need to go.)

I don’t have access to the online forms, and all the information you provide is anonymous. I won’t get any feedback until after I’ve turned in your grades.

There are 10 questions and a textbox for open-ended comments. Those comments are especially helpful. In particular, I’m interested to hear your thoughts about:

  1. class discussion: did you find it valuable? did it increase your understanding of the material? where there things I could have done (or not done) to improve it?
  2. topics/readings: were the readings useful? did you enjoy the topics? were there topics you’d have liked to cover which we did not? topics we covered you’d just as well not have covered?
  3. assignments: I’d like to know what you think both about quantity and quality. Did the assignments push you in good ways to better understand the material? Were they too hard? Were there enough/too many assignments? (There were no exams, only papers: do you think there should have been an exam or two, in addition to papers?)

I’m told that once the SEI form is submitted, you can’t edit it in any way. If you’ve already filled the form out but would like to provide additional anonymous feedback, you can write something up and give it to our department secretary, Michelle Brown (350 University Hall; brown.930@osu.edu). And, of course, I always welcome non-anonymous feedback as well.

The online SEI forms are available until the last day of classes, Friday, December 4. The deadline cannot be extended, so please make sure you respond before finals week.

For more info see http://www.sei.osu.edu

Thanks, David

Final Paper Topics

By David Sanson on November 25, 2009 2:38 PM | No Comments

Instructions

Your paper should be no more than 1000 words (roughly three pages). It should be double-spaced, 12 pt font, with 1 inch margins on all sides (unless you choose to email the paper to me as a plain text email message without formatting).

Papers will be accepted until Midnight, December 10th (Thursday of finals week). Hard copies can be placed in my mailbox in the Philosophy Department Office (University Hall 350). Note that the Department Office usually closes at 5 pm. If you choose to email your paper, please send it as a PDF file, RTF file, or plain text. Include “H101 Final Paper” in the subject line.

If you send it as plain text (my preference), just paste it in to the body of your email message, rather than as a separate attachment.

(Wanna try something new? If you want to indicate simple formatting (bold, italics, section headers, numbered and unnumbered lists, blockquotes) in plain text, use markdown. If you need superscripts, subscripts, and footnotes use pandoc’s extended markdown. The rules are easy to learn and easy to use. Text formatted in this way is easy to read as is and it can easily be transformed into html, latex, pdf, rtf, odt, etc. using free tools. It is a better way to write!)

Prompts

  1. Lay out the problem posed by the possibility of “fission” for any account of personal identity and defend your preferred solution. Along the way, you need to engage with either Parfit or Lewis, either by comparing and contrasting your solution with their solution and explaining why your solution is preferable, or by considering an objection they raise to your solution and providing your response. (If your preferred solution is Parfit’s solution, then you will need to engage with Lewis; if it is Lewis’s solution, you’ll need to engage with Parfit; if it is neither Parfit’s nor Lewis’s solution, then you are free to engage with either).

  2. Lay out the problem posed by Tibbles and Tibs and your preferred solution. Along the way, you should engage with Wiggins, Burke, or Chisholm. (See prompt 1 for ideas about how this engagement might go.)

  3. Lay out the problem posed by the Ship of Theseus, and your preferred solution. Along the way, you should engage with Wiggins, Burke, or Chisholm. (Again, see prompt 1 for ideas about how this engagement might go.)
